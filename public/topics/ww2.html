World War II

America's transformation into a global superpower

American Neutrality and Path to War (1939-1941)

When World War II began in Europe in September 1939, the United States initially maintained a policy of neutrality. However, President Roosevelt believed that a Nazi victory would threaten American security, and he gradually moved the country toward supporting the Allies while trying to avoid direct military involvement.

Key Steps Toward Involvement

  • Cash and Carry (1939): Allowed Allies to purchase American arms if they paid cash and transported goods themselves
  • Destroyers for Bases Deal (1940): The US traded 50 aging destroyers to Britain in exchange for land rights to build bases
  • Lend-Lease Act (March 1941): Allowed the US to "lend" war materials to any nation deemed vital to American defense
  • Atlantic Charter (August 1941): Joint declaration by Roosevelt and Churchill outlining post-war aims
  • Undeclared Naval War (Fall 1941): US Navy escorted British ships and exchanged fire with German submarines

"Yesterday, December 7, 1941—a date which will live in infamy—the United States of America was suddenly and deliberately attacked by naval and air forces of the Empire of Japan."

President Franklin D. Roosevelt, War Message to Congress, December 8, 1941

Pearl Harbor and American Entry into the War

The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, shattered American isolationism and brought the United States fully into World War II. The surprise attack devastated the Pacific Fleet, killing 2,403 Americans and wounding 1,178. The next day, Congress declared war on Japan, and on December 11, Germany and Italy declared war on the United States.

Pearl Harbor Statistics

  • 2,403 Americans killed, 1,178 wounded
  • 18 ships sunk or severely damaged, including 8 battleships
  • 188 aircraft destroyed
  • The USS Arizona memorial remains the final resting place for 1,102 sailors and Marines

Mobilizing for Total War (1941-1945)

World War II transformed the American economy and society. The massive industrial mobilization finally ended the Great Depression and reshaped American society in fundamental ways:

Economic Transformation

  • GDP doubled between 1939 and 1945
  • Unemployment fell from 14% to less than 2%
  • Manufacturing output increased by 300%
  • The US produced 300,000 aircraft, 2.4 million trucks, 634,000 jeeps, 88,000 tanks, and 40 billion bullets
  • War costs totaled $304 billion (over $4 trillion in today's dollars)

Social Changes

  • 16.1 million Americans served in the armed forces
  • 6 million women entered the workforce ("Rosie the Riveter")
  • African American migration from South to Northern industrial centers accelerated
  • Labor unions grew to include over 35% of the workforce
  • Population shifted to urban and suburban areas, particularly on the West Coast

The Home Front

The war effort required significant changes in American domestic life:

Aspect Changes
Government Control The War Production Board controlled industrial production; Office of Price Administration rationed consumer goods
Financing War bonds raised $156 billion; income tax became a mass tax through "pay-as-you-go" withholding
Women Filled critical roles in industry, government, and the military; "Rosie the Riveter" became an iconic symbol
Civil Rights A. Philip Randolph threatened a march on Washington, leading to Executive Order 8802 banning discrimination in defense industries
Japanese Americans Executive Order 9066 led to the internment of 120,000 Japanese Americans, two-thirds of whom were US citizens

Exam Focus: Turning Points

Significant American turning points in World War II include Pearl Harbor (bringing the US into the war), the Battle of Midway (June 1942, turning the tide in the Pacific), D-Day (June 6, 1944, opening the Western Front in Europe), and the development of the atomic bomb (ending the war with Japan). For high marks, be prepared to assess the relative importance of these turning points and how they shaped both the war's outcome and America's position in the post-war world.

Military Campaigns

The United States fought a two-front war against Japan in the Pacific and Germany/Italy in Europe and North Africa:

Pacific War Key Battles

  • Battle of Midway (June 1942): Decisive American naval victory that halted Japanese expansion
  • "Island Hopping" Campaign (1943-1945): Strategy to capture strategic islands while bypassing heavily fortified Japanese positions
  • Battles of Iwo Jima and Okinawa (1945): Bloody battles that demonstrated the potential cost of invading Japan

European War Key Contributions

  • Operation Torch (November 1942): Allied invasion of North Africa
  • Italian Campaign (1943-1945): Allied invasion of Sicily and Italy
  • D-Day (June 6, 1944): Allied invasion of Normandy with over 73,000 American troops
  • Battle of the Bulge (December 1944-January 1945): Last major German offensive on the Western Front
  • Meeting at the Elbe (April 1945): American and Soviet forces link up in Germany

The Manhattan Project and Atomic Bombings

Fearing that Nazi Germany might develop an atomic weapon, the United States launched the top-secret Manhattan Project in 1942 to develop nuclear weapons. By 1945, Germany had surrendered, but the project continued with a focus on ending the war with Japan.

On August 6, 1945, the United States dropped an atomic bomb on Hiroshima, Japan, killing approximately 80,000 people instantly. Three days later, a second bomb was dropped on Nagasaki. On August 15, Japan surrendered, ending World War II.

Historical Debate: The Decision to Use Atomic Weapons

Arguments Supporting the Decision

  • Saved lives by avoiding an invasion of Japan that could have cost 250,000-1,000,000 Allied casualties and millions of Japanese lives
  • Ended the war quickly, preventing further death from conventional bombing, starvation, and fighting
  • Japanese militarism had shown unwillingness to surrender despite conventional bombing

Arguments Questioning the Decision

  • Japan was already defeated and seeking peace terms
  • The bombs were used primarily to intimidate the Soviet Union as the Cold War began
  • Diplomatic alternatives like allowing the emperor to remain were not fully explored

Impact and Legacy

World War II transformed the United States and its position in the world:

Key Impacts

  • Global Power: The US emerged as the world's leading military and economic power
  • Economic Prosperity: The war ended the Depression and launched the post-war economic boom
  • International Institutions: The US led the creation of the United Nations, World Bank, and IMF
  • Civil Rights: The war experience accelerated calls for racial equality
  • Cold War: US-Soviet wartime alliance dissolved into the Cold War rivalry
  • GI Bill: Provided education and housing benefits to veterans, expanding the middle class

Key Figures

Franklin D. Roosevelt

President during most of the war, architect of the Allied grand strategy; died in April 1945

Harry S. Truman

Succeeded Roosevelt, made the decision to use atomic weapons against Japan

General Dwight D. Eisenhower

Supreme Commander of Allied Forces in Europe; oversaw the D-Day invasion

General Douglas MacArthur

Commander of Allied forces in the Pacific; later accepted Japan's surrender

J. Robert Oppenheimer

Scientific director of the Manhattan Project; known as the "father of the atomic bomb"
