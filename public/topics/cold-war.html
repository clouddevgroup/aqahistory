The Cold War

The global struggle between democracy and communism

Origins of the Cold War (1945-1947)

The Cold War emerged from the ashes of World War II as former allies—the United States and Soviet Union—became rivals for global influence. Despite fighting together against Nazi Germany, these superpowers had fundamentally different political, economic, and ideological systems that led to growing mistrust and competition.

Key Factors in the Origins of the Cold War

  • Ideological Differences: American capitalism and democracy versus Soviet communism and authoritarianism
  • Wartime Conferences: Growing tensions at Yalta (February 1945) and Potsdam (July-August 1945) over the post-war order
  • Soviet Control of Eastern Europe: Stalin established communist governments in Poland, Hungary, Bulgaria, and other Eastern European countries
  • Atomic Monopoly: American possession of nuclear weapons initially gave the US strategic advantage
  • Security Concerns: Soviet fear of invasion after suffering 27 million deaths in World War II; American fear of communist expansion

"From Stettin in the Baltic to Trieste in the Adriatic, an iron curtain has descended across the Continent."

Winston Churchill, "Iron Curtain" speech, March 5, 1946

Containment and the Truman Doctrine (1947-1949)

By 1947, the cooperation of the war years had transformed into open rivalry. The United States developed a policy of "containment"—preventing the further spread of communism—which guided American foreign policy for decades.

Key Policies and Events

  • Truman Doctrine (March 1947): Promised American support to "free peoples" resisting "subjugation," initially providing aid to Greece and Turkey
  • Marshall Plan (1948-1952): Provided over $13 billion in economic aid to rebuild Western Europe and create strong allies against communism
  • Berlin Blockade and Airlift (1948-1949): Soviet blockade of West Berlin led to a massive American-led airlift of supplies
  • NATO (1949): North Atlantic Treaty Organization formed as a military alliance against potential Soviet aggression
  • Soviet Atomic Bomb (1949): The USSR successfully tested its first nuclear weapon, ending the American monopoly

1947

Truman Doctrine announced: "It must be the policy of the United States to support free peoples who are resisting attempted subjugation"

The Korean War (1950-1953)

The Cold War turned hot in Korea when North Korean troops, backed by the Soviet Union and China, invaded South Korea in June 1950. President Truman, viewing this as a test of containment, committed American forces under UN authorization to defend South Korea.

Phase Events
North Korean Invasion
(June-September 1950)		 North Korean forces pushed South Korean and American troops to the Pusan Perimeter in the southeastern corner of Korea
UN Counteroffensive
(September-November 1950)		 General MacArthur's amphibious landing at Inchon reversed the tide; UN forces pushed north toward the Chinese border
Chinese Intervention
(November 1950-January 1951)		 300,000 Chinese "volunteers" entered the war, pushing UN forces back below the 38th parallel
Stalemate and Armistice
(1951-1953)		 War settled into a bloody stalemate near the original border; armistice signed July 27, 1953

Significance of the Korean War

  • First major "hot war" of the Cold War era
  • Established the pattern of limited warfare under the nuclear umbrella
  • Led to massive expansion of American defense spending (NSC-68)
  • Reinforced the policy of containment and the American commitment to defend allies
  • Created lasting political division in Korea that continues today
  • 5 million casualties (including 36,000 American deaths)

McCarthyism and the Red Scare (1950-1954)

As the Cold War intensified abroad, fear of communist infiltration grew at home. Senator Joseph McCarthy exploited these fears, claiming to have lists of communists working in the State Department and other government agencies. Though he never substantiated his claims, his accusations ruined many careers and created a climate of suspicion.

Domestic Anti-Communist Measures

  • House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) investigations
  • Hollywood blacklist of suspected communist sympathizers
  • Loyalty oaths required for government employees
  • Espionage cases (Alger Hiss, Rosenbergs)
  • McCarran Internal Security Act (1950)

Impacts of McCarthyism

  • Thousands lost jobs in government, education, entertainment
  • Chilling effect on free speech and political discourse
  • Erosion of civil liberties for perceived national security
  • Legitimization of anti-communist foreign policy
  • McCarthy's downfall during televised Army-McCarthy hearings (1954)

Exam Focus: Historical Interpretations

The origins of the Cold War remain debated among historians. Traditional/Orthodox historians (1950s-60s) blamed Soviet expansionism, while Revisionist historians (1960s-70s) emphasized American economic imperialism. Post-revisionist historians (1970s-present) recognize both sides' contributions to the conflict and stress miscommunication and mutual fear. Post-Cold War scholarship using Soviet archives has provided new insights into Stalin's decision-making. For high marks, acknowledge these different interpretations in your answers.

Eisenhower and the Nuclear Arms Race (1953-1961)

President Dwight D. Eisenhower's administration continued containment policies while expanding America's nuclear arsenal. Eisenhower's "New Look" policy emphasized nuclear deterrence as a cost-effective alternative to maintaining large conventional forces.

Key Developments Under Eisenhower

  • "New Look" Policy: Relied on nuclear weapons and the threat of "massive retaliation" to deter communist aggression
  • Brinkmanship: Willingness to go to the brink of war to achieve objectives
  • CIA Covert Operations: Secret interventions in Iran (1953) and Guatemala (1954) to overthrow governments perceived as pro-communist
  • Domino Theory: Belief that if one nation fell to communism, neighboring states would follow
  • Interstate Highway System: Built partly for national defense to evacuate cities and move military equipment

The Nuclear Arms Race

Both superpowers rapidly developed and expanded their nuclear arsenals, creating the doctrine of Mutual Assured Destruction (MAD)—the idea that neither side would attack because both would be destroyed in the ensuing nuclear exchange.

Year Key Nuclear Developments
1949 Soviet Union tests its first atomic bomb
1952 United States tests first hydrogen bomb (1,000 times more powerful than atomic bombs)
1953 Soviet Union tests hydrogen bomb
1957 Soviet Union launches Sputnik, demonstrating missile capability to reach America
1959 First Intercontinental Ballistic Missiles (ICBMs) deployed
1960 First Submarine-Launched Ballistic Missiles (SLBMs) deployed

Key Figures

Harry S. Truman

President (1945-1953) who established containment policy, the Truman Doctrine, and led America into the Korean War

George F. Kennan

Diplomat who articulated the policy of containment in his famous "Long Telegram" and "X Article"

Joseph McCarthy

Senator whose anti-communist investigations gave name to "McCarthyism" era of domestic fear

Dwight D. Eisenhower

President (1953-1961) who continued containment while relying on nuclear deterrence in his "New Look" policy

John Foster Dulles

Secretary of State under Eisenhower who advocated "brinkmanship" and "massive retaliation"
